## 1) Section Patterns

Use these patterns as scaffolds. Keep content-specific terms from the manuscript.

### Introduction

#### Opening the phenomenon
- `Organizations increasingly face [phenomenon], yet existing explanations remain incomplete.`
- `Although prior studies document [stylized fact], we know less about [specific unresolved issue].`

#### Gap and research question
- `This gap matters because [theoretical relevance] and [managerial relevance].`
- `Accordingly, we ask: [research question]?`

#### Contribution preview
- `This study contributes in three ways. First, ... Second, ... Third, ...`
- `Overall, our framework shifts attention from [old lens] to [new lens].`

### Theory / Literature Dialogue

#### Positioning against prior work
- `Prior research has primarily emphasized [A], whereas we theorize [B].`
- `Existing accounts are informative but insufficient for explaining [boundary/context].`

#### Mechanism articulation
- `We propose that [X] influences [Y] through [M], particularly when [boundary condition].`
- `The mechanism operates because [micro-level logic], which aggregates to [higher-level outcome].`

### Methods

#### Design summary
- `We test our arguments using [design] based on [sample/context].`
- `Our empirical setting is appropriate because [fit to theory].`

#### Measures and validity
- `All focal constructs were measured using established scales adapted from [source].`
- `Results from [CFA/reliability checks] indicate acceptable measurement quality.`

### Results

#### Lead with substantive finding
- `Results provide support for Hypothesis 1.`
- `As shown in Table [n], [substantive finding in words] (beta = ..., p < ...).`

#### Robustness and boundary
- `This pattern remains robust across alternative specifications.`
- `The effect is stronger when [moderator], consistent with Hypothesis [n].`

### Discussion

#### What is learned
- `Our findings suggest that [core takeaway], thereby extending [theory stream].`
- `Rather than [common assumption], the evidence indicates [reframed insight].`

#### Implications and limits
- `For managers, these results imply that [actionable implication].`
- `This study has limitations, including [limitation], which opens avenues for future research.`

#### Closing sentence
- `Taken together, this study clarifies how [X] shapes [Y] under [conditions].`

## 2) Precision Lexicon

Choose wording by evidence strength.

### Claim strength ladder

#### Descriptive evidence
- `shows`
- `indicates`
- `documents`

#### Associational inference
- `is associated with`
- `is linked to`
- `correlates with`

#### Mechanism-consistent inference
- `is consistent with the mechanism that`
- `suggests that [M] may explain`
- `provides evidence in line with`

#### Causal language (use only with strong design)
- `increases`
- `reduces`
- `causally affects`

If causal identification is limited, prefer:
- `may influence`
- `appears to affect`
- `is consistent with a causal interpretation`

### Contrast and boundary
- `whereas`
- `in contrast`
- `conditional on`
- `contingent on`
- `particularly when`

### Robustness language
- `robust to alternative specifications`
- `holds when controlling for`
- `remains substantively unchanged`
- `not driven by`

### Contribution language
- `extends`
- `refines`
- `reconciles`
- `qualifies`
- `challenges the assumption that`

Avoid inflated contribution claims:
- avoid `revolutionizes`, `proves once and for all`, `fully resolves`

## 3) Misuse Replacements

### Overclaiming
- Bad: `X proves that Y causes Z.`
- Better: `X provides evidence that Y may causally affect Z.`

- Bad: `Our results confirm the theory.`
- Better: `Our results are consistent with the theory.`

### Vague contribution
- Bad: `This paper fills a gap in the literature.`
- Better: `This paper explains why [specific inconsistency] emerges under [specific condition].`

- Bad: `This study has important implications.`
- Better: `This study has two implications: [implication 1] and [implication 2].`

### Weak transitions
- Bad: `And then we test H2.`
- Better: `Building on this argument, we next test Hypothesis 2.`

- Bad: `There are many studies.`
- Better: `A growing body of work has examined [topic], but evidence remains mixed regarding [issue].`

### Informal or spoken style
- Bad: `a lot of`
- Better: `substantial`, `considerable`

- Bad: `get`, `show up`, `huge`
- Better: `obtain`, `emerge`, `substantial`

### Results reporting
- Bad: `H1 is significant.`
- Better: `Results support H1: [effect in words] (beta = ..., p < ...).`

- Bad: `See Table 2.`
- Better: `As shown in Table 2, [main substantive pattern].`

### Methods wording
- Bad: `We used a questionnaire and did analysis.`
- Better: `We collected survey data and estimated [model], controlling for [key covariates].`

### Discussion wording
- Bad: `Future research can do more work.`
- Better: `Future research may test whether [mechanism] generalizes to [new context].`
